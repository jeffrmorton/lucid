# EEG and Schumann Resonance Correlation

## Background

The Schumann resonances (SR) are extremely low frequency electromagnetic modes of the Earth-ionosphere cavity. The fundamental mode oscillates at approximately 7.83 Hz, with harmonics near 14.3, 20.8, 27.3, and 33.8 Hz. These frequencies overlap with standard EEG bands: the fundamental falls in the alpha/theta boundary, and harmonics align with beta and gamma ranges.

## Published Research

Several studies have investigated potential correlations between SR activity and human EEG:

- **Saroka et al. (2016)**: Reported real-time coherence between SR signals measured at a remote station and human EEG recorded in a shielded room. Found intermittent phase-locking in the 7-8 Hz range, primarily in posterior electrodes.
- **Persinger & Saroka (2015)**: Described spectral power similarities between global SR measurements and resting-state EEG across multiple subjects.
- **Cherry (2002)**: Hypothesized that SR signals could entrain human brain rhythms, though direct experimental evidence was limited.

## Scientific Assessment

The claimed correlations remain controversial. Key concerns:

- **Signal amplitude**: SR electric field strengths at Earth's surface are on the order of 0.1-1 mV/m. At scalp level, these signals are far below EEG noise floors (microvolts). Any coupling mechanism would need extraordinary sensitivity.
- **Confounding variables**: Both SR and EEG alpha power show diurnal variation. Correlation does not imply direct physical coupling; both could be driven by independent geophysical/biological circadian rhythms.
- **Replication**: Independent replication of the coherence findings by groups outside the original research team is limited.
- **No established mechanism**: There is no widely accepted biophysical mechanism by which sub-millivolt global EM fields would phase-lock with cortical oscillations.

The scientific consensus treats SR-EEG correlation as an interesting observation requiring further investigation, not an established phenomenon.
